Mebendazole and fenbendazole are **very close** chemically and functionally—they belong to the same class of drugs called **benzimidazoles**, a family of antiparasitic (anthelmintic) medications that work primarily by binding to β-tubulin and disrupting microtubule formation in parasites (and potentially in cancer cells).

### Chemical Structure Similarity
Both share a core **benzimidazole ring** structure (a fused benzene and imidazole ring) with a carbamate group at position 2, which is key to their mechanism. The main difference is in the substituent at position 5 of the benzimidazole ring:
- **Mebendazole**: Has a **benzoyl** (C₆H₅C=O) group → Chemical name: methyl 5-benzoyl-1H-benzimidazol-2-ylcarbamate. Molecular formula: C₁₆H₁₃N₃O₃.
- **Fenbendazole**: Has a **phenylthio** (C₆H₅S-) group → Chemical name: methyl 5-(phenylthio)-1H-benzimidazol-2-ylcarbamate. Molecular formula: C₁₅H₁₃N₃O₂S.

This makes them structural analogs (very similar but not identical), differing by one atom/group swap (carbonyl vs. sulfur linkage). Their overall shape, size, and binding properties to tubulin are highly comparable, which is why they have overlapping effects.

### Functional and Mechanistic Closeness
- **Antiparasitic action** → Nearly identical: Both inhibit microtubule polymerization in parasites more effectively than in mammalian cells (due to differences in tubulin affinity).
- **Potential anticancer effects** (preclinical data) → Both show activity by disrupting microtubules in cancer cells, inhibiting cell division, reducing glucose uptake (via effects on GLUT transporters/hexokinase in some studies), and inducing cell death. Some research suggests mebendazole may have slightly higher tubulin binding affinity and potency in certain models, while fenbendazole might have advantages in glycolysis inhibition in drug-resistant cells—but these are subtle differences, and both are considered promising in lab/animal studies for cancers like glioblastoma, colorectal, and others.
- They often appear together in drug repurposing screens for cancer (e.g., alongside albendazole and others in the benzimidazole family).

### Key Differences (Beyond Chemistry)
- **Approval and use** — Mebendazole is FDA-approved and widely used in humans for parasitic infections (e.g., pinworms, hookworms). Fenbendazole is veterinary-only (e.g., for dogs, livestock) and not approved for human use.
- **Research for cancer** — Mebendazole has progressed further into human clinical trials (Phase 1/2 for brain tumors like glioblastoma, showing safety and some signals of benefit). Fenbendazole remains mostly preclinical/animal data, with anecdotal human use (e.g., popularized by case reports like Joe Tippens) but limited formal human safety/efficacy data.
- **Pharmacokinetics** — Slight variations in absorption, metabolism (e.g., fenbendazole metabolizes to oxfendazole), and bioavailability, but both have poor water solubility, often requiring special formulations for better effect.

In short: They are **extremely close**—essentially "cousins" in the same drug family with nearly interchangeable mechanisms for antiparasitic and potential anticancer purposes. The biggest practical gap is human approval and clinical evidence, where mebendazole has a clear edge for people. Neither is an approved cancer treatment yet; any use for that remains experimental/off-label.
